I About Elisabeth Daem (b. 1990, Brussels) is a commercial & IP law lawyer, with almost 8 years' experience advising and assisting clients in various industries, however with a specific interest in the creative industries (such as fashion, -graphic- design, music, film and fine arts). She practises in all main fields of general business law as well as in the field of IP law, handling (strategic) advisory, contractual and (complex) litigation work. In addition, as a part-time assistant at the KULeuven (at the Centre for IT and IP), she regularly publishes legal articles and blogposts, speaks at seminars/webinars and is a member of a number of (local) industry associations such as BMM and ABA/BVA.

I Aim Elisabeth's aim is to provide straightforward, pragmatic legal assistance to creatives, so that they can focus on the substance of their work and the realisation of their creations. Having a background in art history herself, she is constantly surrounded by creatives in her (private) network. She supports her creative network (from individuals and start-ups to SME's) by providing legal advice, reviewing and negotiating contracts and legal assistance during legal proceedings.

I Capabilities Providing legal advice regarding (i) the protection of your creation, copyright, portrait rights, (ii) registering your trademark, drawings or models; (iii) the validity of your advertisement; (iv) the implementation in Belgian law of recent legislation such as the AVMS Directive or DSM Directive; (v) the reform of the copyright tax regime; etc. Assisting in contract review/negotiations regarding (i) the transfer or use of your intellectual property rights (freelance/employment context); (ii) distribution/franchising/agency agreements; (iii) terms and conditions (incl. terms of use for websites); (iv) NDAs and confidentiality agreements; (v) all kinds of commercial collaboration agreements; etc. Providing legal assistance in legal proceedings concerning (i) the use of an identical or similar trademark/company name for identical or similar services/products (and thereby creating a likelihood of confusion); (ii) unlawful reproduction of copyrighted works (especially in the fashion and design sector); (iii) unfair market practices (where e.g. confusion or deception is created between two services of two different companies); etc.
